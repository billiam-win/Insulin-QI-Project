About this project

Intraoperative glucose management in the General Operating Rooms is currently variable and provider-driven, with no standardized workflow for recognizing or acting on out-of-range blood glucose values. A root cause analysis identified protocol inaccessibility and cognitive load as key barriers to adherence.

This reference is part of a 15-week quality improvement initiative (Fall 2026) aimed at improving anesthesia provider adherence to the institutional intraoperative glucose management protocol — specifically, initiating protocol-concordant care for blood glucose values >180 mg/dL and <100 mg/dL — to reduce intraoperative hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia events among adult non-cardiac surgical patients.

Glucose targets & when to use insulin infusion

Targets

  • ADA target range: 80–180 mg/dL. Many clinicians prefer the upper end (140–180 mg/dL) under anesthesia to reduce hypoglycemia risk.
  • Use IV regular insulin for all intraoperative correction — subcutaneous absorption is unreliable under anesthesia.

Preoperative glucose thresholds

  • BG >180 mg/dL: must be treated or rechecked within 90 minutes.
  • BG <100 mg/dL: must be treated or rechecked within 90 minutes.

IV bolus sliding scale

  • Appropriate for shorter procedures requiring correction of hyperglycemia.
  • Not appropriate for: Type 1 diabetes, labile glucose, or frequent hypoglycemia. Patients with significant insulin resistance should generally be managed with a continuous infusion.

When to start an infusion

  • 3 consecutive BG readings >180 mg/dL, OR
  • 2 consecutive BG readings >200 mg/dL
  • Strongly recommended for refractory hyperglycemia and/or prolonged, physiologically stressful surgery.
  • Special considerations:
    • Prime tubing with ≥25 mL of insulin from the bag before initiating, unless using low-sorbing tubing.
    • Patients with severe renal dysfunction may have impaired insulin clearance — consider starting lower and titrating more slowly.
    • Monitor electrolytes/blood gas periodically when correcting significant hyperglycemia; potassium repletion may be required.
Hypoglycemia recognition & treatment

Recognition

  • Hypoglycemia is difficult to detect in anesthetized patients. Suspect it with unexplained diaphoresis, arrhythmias, or other cardiac events. Vigilant glucose monitoring is the primary defense.
  • In Type 1 (insulin-dependent) diabetics, do not disrupt basal insulin — this risks ketoacidosis. If glucose trends down, initiate a dextrose infusion rather than holding basal.

Treatment (<70 mg/dL)

  • Treat with IV dextrose:
    • 12.5–25 g IV dextrose (25–50 mL of D50W), OR
    • D10 125 mL IV bolus, or dextrose equivalent per pharmacy
  • Recheck BG every 15 minutes; repeat intervention until BG >70 mg/dL.
  • Consider initiating or increasing a dextrose infusion for persistent hypoglycemia.
Titration rules

Titration

  1. Use the titration table to find the starting rate based on the patient's blood glucose value and scale. If the patient's glucose is greater than 390 mg/dL, consult the provider to determine desired interventions and starting point on the titration scale.
  2. Move between scales A, B, and C as follows:
    • Two consecutive BG readings >150 mg/dL on the same scale: increase to next scale (A → B → C)
    • BG <120 mg/dL: decrease to next scale (C → B → A)
  3. If patient made NPO OR blood glucose decreases by >100 mg/dL in 1 hour: decrease insulin drip by 50% and notify provider.
  4. If blood glucose values are within desired range (100–180 mg/dL) for 2 hours, then blood glucose can be checked every 2 hours. Return to every 1 hour checks if blood glucose is out of goal range (<100 mg/dL or >180 mg/dL).

Hold and hypoglycemia guidance

Hold and hypoglycemia

  • For any blood glucose <90 mg/dL, hold infusion and recheck blood glucose in 30 minutes then every 1 hour for 4 hours. Restart insulin drip on Scale A if blood glucose is >150 mg/dL during that time.
  • For any blood glucose 70 to 80 mg/dL, hold infusion, notify provider, and recheck blood glucose in 30 minutes then every 1 hour for 4 hours. Restart insulin drip on Scale A if blood glucose is >150 mg/dL during that time.
  • For any blood glucose <70 mg/dL, immediately stop insulin infusion and initiate hypoglycemia management. Notify provider of the hypoglycemia, wait until 4 hours have elapsed after hypoglycemic episode (<70 mg/dL) then follow the insulin infusion initiation guidance in section B.1 of the policy.
